On Friday, December 19th, the Governor of New York, Kathy Hochul, vetoed Assembly Bill 4873, which would have mandated two-person train operation on all New York City Subway services. While transit unions took this as a personal attack against blue-collared New Yorkers who work the subway, transit advocacy groups and the MTA cheered the veto as a commitment from the governor to keeping transit costs sensible. Today, we'll break down exactly what the bill entailed, and why it was such a ground-breaking decision.

## The New York City Subway & Its Train Operations

The New York City Subway is America's busiest rapid transit system, stretching 472 stations across 4 of the city's five boroughs - the Staten Island Railway (SIR), as per the MTA, is not included under the umbrella of the NYC Subway. Carrying 2 billion people in 2024, the 24/7 subway system requires somewhere between 30,000 and 50,000 personnel, as a rough estimate, to keep the system operational, safe, and efficient; this is out of a total of 70,000 people who work for the MTA overall, which includes the Long Island Rail Road, Metro-North and the bus system. 

Most trains on the system are consisted of about 10 cars, and are typically staffed by an operator who drives the train, and a conductor, situated at the middle, who makes announcements and safely opens and closes doors - you might see the conductor sticking their head of the window around the middle of the train at stations. Exceptions to this include the G train and the 42nd Street Shuttle, which only require an operator thanks to its shorter length. This technique is known as one person train operation, or OPTO - according to <a href="https://transitcosts.com/Train_Operations.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">a study completed by New York University</a>, under 7% of the 400 included commuter rail & transit systems utilize two personnel on trains - the majority employ OPTO, or have zero-person train operation through automation. 

## The Bill: Assembly Bill 4873

Assembly Bill 4873 was a bill that supported mandating two-person train operation across all New York City Subway services - the specific language was that all MTA trains of two or more cars should include at least 2 personnel. The bill was sponsored by Democrat representative Monique Chandler-Waterman of Brooklyn, and was fiercely endorsed by transit unions who represented workers on the NYC Subway, citing safety for the operators and a liability issue if they weren't present. While the current contract between the MTA and the Transport Workers Union (TWU) mandates two-person train operation on most services, this bill would have mandated two-person train operation across the system.

The bill swiftly passed both houses of the New York State Legislature, passing the State Assembly 144-0, and the State Senate by 57-2. The last step was for the bill to land on the desk of Governor Hochul, who would ultimately decide its fate.

## Hochul's Veto and The Reactions

On Friday, December 19th, Kathy Hochul vetoed Assembly Bill 4873. In her memo, she wrote 

_<center>"The bill would require MTA New York City Transit (NYCT) to discontinue its current safe One Person Train Operation (OPTO) program, which is already subject to, and limited in scope by, collective bargaining agreements. The bill would cost as much as $10 million annually, reducing service, and limiting the MTA's ability to benefit from capital investments in modern rolling stock and signals. OPTO is safe; NYCT has successfully run OPTO for over two decades, and the program is widely utilized in mass transit systems throughout the nation and across the globe."</center>_


Upon the news of her veto, the president of the Transport Workers Union, John Samuelsen, wrote a scathing post on X claiming that "blue collar New Yorkers overwhemingly support" the bill and called the veto "futile." John Chiarallo, the president of the local chapter of TWU, TWU Local 100, stated disappointment in Hochul's decision, but remarked that the existing contract prohibits any unsanctioned expansion of OPTO across the system, so the union intends to continue operating trains with two personnel, as agreed upon with the MTA. 

On the other side of the aisle, a coalition of watchdog and business groups thanked Hochul for the veto. Transit advocate groups claimed that the bill was a direct attempt to raise the price of operating transit, going against the goal of cheaper transit that the MTA is striving for. The increased $10 million cost of two-person train operation would require the MTA to dip into their capital budget, siphoning money away from impending, necessary capital projects. A <a href="https://reinventalbany.org/2025/12/groups-thank-governor-hochul-for-vetoing-bill-prohibiting-mta-from-using-one-person-train-operation/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">statement</a> from five of these groups said the bill would affect the MTA's efforts to provide "world-class public transit" by "raising MTA operating costs and constraining the MTA’s ability to implement modern operating methods, adopt new technologies, and provide better service for riders.”

The vetoing of this bill also opens the door for the MTA to move further and further towards OPTO, reducing labor costs and aligning themselves with what is widely seen as a standard across the world.
